What is LiRRiL

LiRRiL is an app based on scientific research that helps non-native English speakers who are experiencing problems differentiating L and R sounds. Researchers at Carnegie Melon University conducted an experiment on teaching native Japanese speakers differentiate between L and R sounds. Participants were played words with /l/r/ ambiguity, such as ‘play’ – ‘pray’, or ‘long’ – ‘wrong’, and asked to pick the word they heard. At first the participants were not shown whether their answers were correct or wrong, and they scored an average of 50%, which is no better than mere guessing.

Why Japanese speakers have difficulties differentiating western L-R sounds

Lack of L and R sounds in Japanese language

In Japanese there are no distinctive L and R sounds as we have them in most other languages. Japanese, on the other hand, has a liquid flap consonant that varies between a lateral [ɺ] and central [ɾ] sounds 1 [wikipedia]. In western languages, such as English for example, we have two very distinctive sounds for L and R, which linguists denote with /l/ and [ɹ]. This leads to that native Japanese speakers, who haven't learned a language, such as English, at a very young age will have difficulties hearing and producing western L and R sounds accurately. The confusion of L and R sounds is often referred to as lallation.

The brain tunes-in on language specific sounds at a very young age

A language is composed of many parts. We speak in sentences, which are made up of words. The ordering of words within a sentence is governed by the grammar used by a particular language. Words, on the other hand, are composed of phonetic sounds. There are about 6500 different languages in the world, which use about 150 different phonetic sounds. No language of course contains all 150 sounds, in English for example, we use only 44 phonemes.

It is obvious to everybody that you need to know the vocabulary of a language, or at least a few words, before you can start making sentences. Research has confirmed though that before we can learn the words of a language, the first step is to learn the sounds which make up the words. This is easily done by the brain when it is still in an early development stage, but later on in life, the brain diverts its energy to other parts and picking up foreign sounds becomes much harder. Teach the developed brain new phonemes

Although the brain looses its ability to learn new phonemes automatically at an very early stage, not all hope is lost. Recent research has uncovered the secret to teach the developed brain how to learn new sounds [ Lively & Pisoni , McClelland, Fiez & McCandliss ]. Several techniques were tried out in these studies, but only one technique proved to work. The secret to learning new sounds is to provide an instant feedback loop. This means that whenever you submit an answer to whether you heard an L or R sound, you are given immediate feedback on whether your answer was correct or wrong. Through the immediate feedback, your brain will develop the mechanisms it needs to accurately distinguish between these sounds.

In the above study ( McClelland, Fiez & McCandliss ) a control group was used to confirm the results are genuine. The control group was given the same task, to distinguish between L and R ambiguous words, but they did not receive the immediate feedback whether their answer was right or wrong. They were only told after the experiment which answers were correct and which were wrong. Because they did not receive the immediate feedback, they made no improvements and retained an accuracy of 50%.

The group that received the immediate feedback after each submitted answer, was able to score an average of 80% accuracy after just 30 minutes of training! Yet another study [Bradlow, Akahane-Yamada, Pisoni, Tohkura] found that distinguishing L and R sounds also improves the speakers ability to produce/pronounce these words. The study also showed that the newly learned ability stays with you for the long-term. After 6 months without practicing their accuracy began to slightly drop again.
